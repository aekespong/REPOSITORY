# A Modern Approach to Using AI

### From Individual Prompting to Strategic Integration

Artificial intelligence is often introduced as a tool. A chatbot. A code assistant. A productivity enhancer.

That framing is too narrow.

AI is not simply a new tool in the toolbox. It represents a shift in how we think, collaborate, design, and build. Organizations that treat AI as an add-on will see incremental improvements. Organizations that integrate AI into their way of working will experience structural change.

A modern approach to AI is therefore not about attending a course, nor about selecting the “best” model. It is about developing new working methods, shared practices, and secure integration patterns that make AI part of everyday value creation.

This article outlines three layers of a modern AI approach:

1.Individual AI literacy through practice
2.Collaborative AI embedded in team workflows
3.Enterprise-grade AI integration with governance and control

Together, these form an AI operating model rather than isolated experimentation.

--- 

## You Don’t Learn AI From Courses

Traditional technology adoption often starts with training programs. AI is different.

You can attend seminars about prompt engineering, model capabilities, or generative AI trends. But true AI competence does not emerge from theory. It develops through practice — in real work, under real constraints, with real stakeholders.

AI is contextual. Its effectiveness depends on:

- The domain knowledge of the user
- The clarity of the problem
- The quality of input context
- The ability to iterate and refine

Because of this, AI mastery is personal and experiential. Each professional develops patterns that fit their way of thinking and their business context.

More importantly, AI maturity is not an individual achievement. It becomes valuable when it is shared and embedded in team practices.

--- 

## From Solo Prompting to Collaborative AI

In many organizations, AI usage begins informally:

- Individuals experiment with ChatGPT.
- Developers use coding assistants in isolation.
- Consultants generate drafts or summaries privately.
- Knowledge remains personal.

This stage can improve individual productivity, but it does not transform the organization. Insights are not shared. Prompting techniques are not standardized. Learning remains fragmented.

A modern approach moves beyond solo usage toward collaborative AI.

### AI in the Room

Collaborative AI (CollabAI) means embedding AI directly into team interactions:

- During workshops
- In customer interviews
- In architecture discussions
- In backlog refinement
- In solution design sessions

Instead of summarizing after the fact, AI participates in real time:

- Structuring insights during interviews
- Generating alternative solution patterns
- Identifying missing assumptions
- Suggesting risks and dependencies
- Producing structured documentation instantly

When teams are co-located — physically or digitally — AI becomes a shared cognitive assistant.

This shift produces several effects:

1.**Shared learning**

 Teams develop collective prompting patterns and shared practices.
2.**Business alignment**

 AI is applied in context, not abstract exercises.
3.**Faster iteration cycles**

 Ideas can be generated, tested, and refined immediately.
4.**Reduced documentation lag**

 Workshop outcomes are structured as they happen.

The key insight is this: AI is most powerful when it augments collaboration, not when it replaces it.

--- 

## AI as a Knowledge Engine, Not Just a Code Generator

Many AI initiatives focus primarily on code generation. While this delivers measurable productivity gains, it addresses only part of the value potential.

Organizations struggle not only with writing code — they struggle with maintaining structured knowledge:

- Architecture descriptions
- Information models
- Process documentation
- API contracts
- Decision records
- Governance artifacts

Documentation is often outdated, inconsistent, or neglected because maintaining it is time-consuming and rarely prioritized.

A modern AI approach includes a generic mechanism for generating and updating structured Markdown documentation that supports the team continuously.

### Living Documentation

Instead of static files that decay over time, AI can:

- Generate structured templates based on context
- Update documentation when code changes
- Refactor architectural descriptions
- Align information models across teams
- Identify inconsistencies

The goal is not to automate documentation blindly. It is to reduce friction so that structured knowledge becomes a natural output of work.

This approach shifts documentation from a manual burden to a semi-automated knowledge flow. Over time, this strengthens architectural integrity, governance maturity, and onboarding efficiency.

AI becomes a knowledge engine — not merely a conversational assistant.

## Enterprise AI Integration: Architecture Matters

As AI usage expands, a critical question arises:

How do we integrate AI responsibly, securely, and strategically?

Ad-hoc usage through public SaaS tools may be sufficient for experimentation. However, when AI processes proprietary code, sensitive data, or regulated information, architecture decisions become essential.

A modern integration architecture for AI should be guided by five principles:

1.Data sovereignty
2.Security by design
3.Model flexibility
4.Governance and cost control
5.Strategic autonomy

### Example Architecture

A structured enterprise AI solution can consist of the following layers:

#### Developer Interface

Developers work in their existing environments using an integrated interface (e.g., OpenCode). AI assistance becomes part of the normal development workflow rather than an external tool.

#### Model Abstraction Layer

A layer such as liteLLM abstracts access to underlying AI models. This decouples the interface from specific model providers.

Benefits include:

- Flexibility to switch models
- Reduced dependency on a single vendor
- Controlled routing and logging
- Centralized policy enforcement

#### AI Models

Advanced models such as Anthropic Sonnet or Opus provide strong reasoning and coding capabilities. These models enhance code quality, support refactoring, and assist with debugging and architectural reasoning.

#### Infrastructure Hosting

Crucially, the models are hosted within the organization’s own Azure cloud environment in the EU.

This design ensures:

- Sensitive code never leaves the controlled environment
- Compliance with EU data protection regulations
- Reduced geopolitical and jurisdictional risk
- Clear governance over logging, retention, and access control

--- 

## Strategic Benefits for Management

From a management perspective, this architecture delivers several advantages.

### Enhanced Data Security and EU Sovereignty

Hosting models within an EU-based cloud environment ensures that proprietary assets remain under organizational control. This significantly reduces regulatory risk and aligns with data protection requirements.

In industries with strict compliance demands, this is not a luxury — it is a prerequisite.

### Increased Developer Productivity

Integrated AI assistance:

- Accelerates development cycles
- Improves code consistency
- Reduces repetitive tasks
- Enhances debugging efficiency

Over time, these gains compound and impact time-to-market and cost structures.

### Strategic Control and Flexibility

By controlling the infrastructure and abstracting model access:

- The organization avoids dependence on external inference platforms.
- Switching or testing new models becomes simpler.
- Long-term investment risk is reduced.

AI becomes a managed capability rather than a consumer subscription.

## Trade-offs and Operational Considerations

A credible AI strategy also acknowledges its trade-offs.

### Operational Overhead

Running AI infrastructure internally requires:

- Monitoring
- Cost management
- Model updates
- Security governance

This demands expertise and ongoing attention.

### Cost Complexity

Costs include:

- Model usage fees
- Compute and storage in Azure
- Observability and logging infrastructure

Without careful governance, expenses can scale rapidly.

### Model Transparency Limits

Using proprietary models means:

- Limited visibility into internal mechanisms
- Restricted ability to fine-tune model weights
- Dependence on vendor roadmaps

For some use cases, open-source models may complement proprietary ones.

### Not Fully Offline

Even when hosted internally, the solution requires network connectivity to the organization’s cloud. Fully air-gapped environments remain challenging.

These considerations do not invalidate the approach — but they require intentional governance.

## From Tool Adoption to AI Operating Model

A modern approach to AI can be described as a maturity progression.

### Layer 1: Individual AI Literacy

People experiment.

They learn by doing.

They develop intuition through practice.

Training supports this, but practice defines it.

### Layer 2: Collaborative AI

AI becomes part of workshops, interviews, and design sessions.

Teams share patterns and continuously improve usage.

Learning becomes collective.

### Layer 3: Enterprise AI Architecture

AI is integrated securely and strategically.

Data sovereignty is guaranteed.

Governance, cost control, and flexibility are designed in.

At this stage, AI is no longer a tool. It is an organizational capability.

--- 

## The Real Shift

The most significant transformation is not technical.

It is methodological.

AI challenges traditional assumptions about:

- How quickly we can produce structured output
- How knowledge flows within organizations
- How decisions are documented
- How architecture evolves

Organizations that embrace AI as an operating model — not just a productivity enhancer — will build faster learning loops, stronger architectural coherence, and better alignment between business and technology.

The question is not whether to use AI.

The question is how deliberately we design its role in our way of working.

A modern approach to AI is therefore not about choosing the strongest model.

It is about designing how AI becomes part of how we think, collaborate, and build.
